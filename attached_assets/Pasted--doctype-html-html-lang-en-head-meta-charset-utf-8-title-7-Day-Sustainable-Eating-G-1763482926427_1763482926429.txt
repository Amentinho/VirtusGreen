          <h2>Big picture: why food matters</h2>
          <p>
            The global food system — from farming and land-use change to processing, transport and retail — makes up a major share
            of planetary pressure: roughly a quarter to a third of global greenhouse gas emissions depending on the accounting method.
            Agriculture also uses a large share of freshwater and habitable land. That means changes in what and how we eat scale quickly at population level. 
          </p>
          <p class="note">(Key source: Our World in Data, UNEP Food Waste Index; see Sources below.)</p>

          <h2>How impacts arise (clear breakdown)</h2>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Production emissions:</strong> Fertiliser, animal enteric emissions, farm energy and feed production.</li>
            <li><strong>Land-use change:</strong> Clearing forests or draining peat to create pasture or cropland releases large carbon stocks.</li>
            <li><strong>Processing & transport:</strong> Refrigeration, packaging and long-distance shipping add emissions (but often less than production).</li>
            <li><strong>Food waste:</strong> When edible food is wasted, all upstream emissions and resources are effectively lost.</li>
          </ul>

          <h2>Real-world case studies you can cite</h2>

          <div class="example">
            <strong>Too Good To Go — surplus rescue at scale</strong>
            <p class="note">Too Good To Go is a consumer app that connects users to surplus food from retailers and restaurants. In recent impact reporting the organisation
            reports saving tens of millions — and in some years over 100 million — meals from going to waste. This model shows how consumer behaviour + simple tech delivers measurable waste reduction quickly.</p>
          </div>

          <div class="example">
            <strong>Oddbox — rescuing imperfect produce</strong>
            <p class="note">Oddbox works directly with growers to rescue 'too odd' or surplus produce. For example, in a single reported season they rescued hundreds of tonnes of fruit that would otherwise have been wasted.
            The lesson: changing retail acceptance and consumer demand (buying 'ugly' fruit) reduces farm-level loss.</p>
          </div>

          <div class="example">
            <strong>Walmart & IBM Food Trust — traceability to reduce uncertainty</strong>
            <p class="note">Walmart's traceability initiative (with IBM Food Trust/Hyperledger) required leafy-greens suppliers to register trace events,
            enabling rapid tracing back to farms in seconds instead of days. Better traceability reduces recall times, helps target waste reduction,
            and increases shopper trust in product origin.</p>
          </div>

          <h2 style="margin-top:18px">7-Day Expanded Action Plan (detailed — use these in your PDF/landing page)</h2>

          <!-- DAY 1 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 1 — Choose seasonal produce</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Seasonal crops are harvested when climate & soil conditions are optimal, reducing energy needed for greenhouse heating, artificial ripening, and refrigerated storage. They often travel shorter distances and use fewer inputs per unit of food.</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Check seasonality calendars for your country (farmers' markets often list seasonal highlights).</li>
              <li>Buy tomatoes, strawberries and cucumbers in their local season — avoid imported greenhouse-grown out-of-season varieties.</li>
              <li>Shop at your local weekly market one week a month and compare prices — seasonal produce is usually cheaper and tastier.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="note"><strong>Example:</strong> swapping imported, out-of-season tomatoes for locally grown seasonal tomatoes reduces refrigeration and transport needs and typically improves flavor.</p>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 2 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 2 — Reduce food waste with simple meal planning</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Household-level avoidable food waste is a large fraction of global waste (households account for around 60% of consumer-level waste in recent UN reporting). Wasting food wastes the embedded water, land and emissions.</p> 
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Plan 5–7 meals for the week and create a single shopping list to avoid impulse buys.</li>
              <li>Use the "first in, first out" fridge rule: move older items to the front.</li>
              <li>Batch-cook and freeze extras in labelled portions. Freeze ripe fruit for smoothies.</li>
              <li>Use apps or a simple whiteboard on the fridge to list leftovers — make "leftover night" a weekly habit.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="note"><strong>Quick metric:</strong> UN reporting estimates 1.05 billion tonnes of food were wasted at retail, food service, and households in 2022 — reducing household waste is high-leverage. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}</p>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 3 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 3 — Try a plant-based protein swap</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Ruminant meat (beef, lamb) is among the highest-impact foods per kg in CO₂-eq, land and water. Swapping one beef meal per week for plant proteins can produce measurable per-person reductions.</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Start small: replace one meal per week (e.g., beef bolognese → lentil bolognese).</li>
              <li>Try tasty, simple recipes: bean tacos, chickpea curry, tofu stir-fry, lentil shepherd's pie.</li>
              <li>Use dried legumes (cheaper & lower footprint than processed meat alternatives).</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="note"><strong>Example:</strong> Lentils and beans require far less land and water than beef and are inexpensive sources of protein and fiber. (Use per-kg comparison charts when posting visuals.) :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}</p>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 4 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 4 — Check packaging and recyclability</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Some environmental gains from food are undermined by excessive or hard-to-recycle packaging. Minimising unnecessary packaging improves circularity.</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Prefer loose produce over pre-packaged where possible.</li>
              <li>Choose glass or metal for long-life products (they are widely recyclable).</li>
              <li>Avoid black plastics and multilayer film; these are often nonrecyclable in municipal systems.</li>
              <li>Bring your own containers for bulk sections when allowed.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="note">Retailers are piloting reusable packaging and refill stations — highlight local examples to your audience.</p>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 5 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 5 — Prefer local & low-transport foods</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Transport isn't always the largest share of food emissions, but choosing local reduces food-mile emissions (often refrigeration and air freight are the worst offenders) and supports regional resilience.</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Buy fruit & vegetables from local sellers or farmers' markets when in season.</li>
              <li>Prefer produce labelled as locally grown rather than air-freighted items (e.g., berries flown from distant countries).</li>
              <li>Support local dairies and bakeries — these often have lower distribution footprints over the final mile.</li>
            </ul>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 6 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 6 — Understand footprint indicators (CO₂, water, land)</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Learning the three main indicators—carbon (CO₂e), water footprint and land use—lets you make informed tradeoffs (e.g., some foods use little water but more land; others reverse the pattern).</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Look up simple charts comparing kg CO₂e per kg for common foods (beef vs chicken vs legumes vs tofu).</li>
              <li>Use per-portion comparisons (per meal or per 100 g) in social posts to make numbers relatable.</li>
              <li>Remember: context matters — production method, feed, and country change numbers significantly.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="note">Tip: Save a small table on your phone with quick comparisons to use in social posts and conversations.</p>
          </section>

          <!-- DAY 7 -->
          <section>
            <h3>Day 7 — Build a weekly sustainable shopping routine</h3>
            <p><strong>Why:</strong> Habits lock in savings. A short routine reduces waste, impulsive purchases and helps you choose lower-impact foods consistently.</p>
            <p><strong>How to do it (practical):</strong></p>
            <ul>
              <li>Create a single shopping list from your week's meal plan.</li>
              <li>Keep a running “use this first” list on the fridge for items close to expiry.</li>
              <li>Schedule one weekly market visit and one “batch-cook & freeze” session.</li>
              <li>Track one basic metric: number of meals you saved from waste this week (even a small number is progress).</li>
            </ul>
          </section>

          <h2 style="margin-top:18px">Practical content ideas using these examples</h2>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>IG Reel:</strong> "One swap = X kg CO₂e saved" — pick a real per-kg comparison, show the meal swap and a simple call-to-action to download your checklist.</li>
            <li><strong>Carousel:</strong> "How Too Good To Go works" — short explainer plus local sign-up suggestion or partnership idea.</li>
            <li><strong>LinkedIn article:</strong> "Five steps retailers can adopt now" — cite Walmart's traceability rollout and Tesco/retailer waste programs as blueprints.</li>
          </ul>

          <h2 style="margin-top:18px">Short checklist to keep (one page)</h2>
          <ol>
            <li>Buy seasonal produce this week</li>
            <li>Plan meals & freeze extras</li>
            <li>Swap one meat meal for legumes</li>
            <li>Prefer recyclable packaging</li>
            <li>Buy local where possible</li>
            <li>Check quick footprint indicators</li>
            <li>Do a weekly waste audit (1 number)</li>
          </ol>
